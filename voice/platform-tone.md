# Platform Tone Adaptations

Core voice defined in [[brand-voice]]. This file defines how that voice ADAPTS per platform.

The base voice is always: calm intelligence, aspirational intimacy, founder-led authority, emotionally perceptive, direct about industry problems.

What changes: energy level, formality, sentence length, conversational markers, and format expectations.

---

## TikTok

**Energy:** Highest. Most raw and unfiltered version of the voice.
**Register:** Spoken, not written. Write exactly how Faith or Lydia would actually talk to a friend.

- Fast-paced, hook immediately (first 1-2 seconds)
- Short punchy sentences. No filler. Every word earns its spot
- "okay so" "literally" "like" "wait" used naturally as conversational markers
- Can be blunt, frustrated, even a little heated about industry BS
- Emotion is allowed to show. Passion reads as authentic here
- Captions/text overlays mandatory (muted viewing)
- Write TikTok FIRST, then adapt to other platforms

**Example hooks:**
- "okay so you're telling me 67% of people can't find foundation that matches and we're just... accepting that?"
- "the beauty industry really said 'here's 40 shades, figure it out yourself' and called it inclusive"
- "why are we still mixing two bottles together in 2026. genuinely asking"

**What works:** Raw frustration, relatable anger, "wait this is actually insane" energy, behind-the-scenes chaos, founder vulnerability, fast educational drops.

**What fails:** Polished brand speak, scripted-sounding delivery, slow buildups, dense information without visual support.

---

## Instagram

**Energy:** Polished but warm. Most visually refined version of the voice.
**Register:** Elevated casual. Smart friend at a nice dinner, not boardroom presentation.

- Clean, considered, aesthetically aware
- Conversational but never sloppy
- Can go deeper than TikTok on education and context
- Carousels: one idea per slide, max 7-10 words on slide 1
- Captions: two-paragraph structure (hook/teach, then deepen + CTA)
- Brand colors and typography matter here

**Example caption opening:**
"120 billion units of beauty packaging end up in landfills yearly [BeautyMatter 2024]. Most sustainability conversations focus on what happens after you finish a product. Almost nobody talks about the waste created when the product never worked in the first place."

**What works:** Sharp visual-text combos, myth vs truth, consumer behavior insights, editorial observations, carousels that teach, Reels adapted from TikTok (slightly more polished).

**What fails:** Generic brand captions, text that doesn't match visual quality, dense paragraphs, hashtag spam (keep to 3-5 strategic ones), salesy CTAs.

---

## X (Twitter)

**Energy:** Sharpest and most direct. Most provocative version of the voice.
**Register:** Lowercase. Punchy. No fluff.

- Every word must earn its place. Cut ruthlessly
- Hot takes welcome. Industry criticism encouraged
- Lowercase everything (brand standard)
- No hashtags. No links in main tweet (link in reply if needed)
- Sarcasm and dry observations work well here
- This is where media/press discover the brand. Write accordingly
- Threads for depth, single tweets for punches

**Example tweets:**
- "the beauty industry invented 40 shades, watched 67% of people still fail to find a match, and then blamed undertones"
- "mixing two foundations together is not a hack. it's a symptom"
- "billion dollar brands. 40 shades. 67% failure rate. make it make sense"
- "every 'inclusive' foundation launch that still requires you to guess your undertone is just better marketing on the same broken system"

**What works:** Sharp observations, industry criticism, quotable lines, numbers that hit hard, founder POV hot takes, engagement with relevant conversations.

**What fails:** Corporate tone, long explanations, hashtags, links in main tweet, trying to sound nice when the point requires sharpness.

---

## YouTube

**Energy:** Most structured and educational. Authority mode.
**Register:** Expert teaching a class, but still human and conversational.

- Longer, more detailed, step-by-step where appropriate
- SEO-driven platform. Title is everything. Hook in first 5 seconds
- Authority voice with depth and proof
- Can go long on education, context, and founder story
- Evergreen content matters here. This is a search engine
- Shorts: extract best 30-60 second segments from long-form

**Example video structures:**
- "Why 67% of People Can't Find Their Foundation Shade (The Real Reason)" (educational explainer)
- "We Tested 4.5 Million Shade Combinations. Here's What We Learned." (data-driven)
- "From Sephora Floor to Beauty Tech Founder: Our Story" (founder narrative)
- "What Nobody Tells You About Foundation Matching" (myth-busting)

**What works:** Strong titles, clear chapter structure, educational depth, founder authenticity, B-roll of product/prototype, before/after demonstrations.

**What fails:** Slow intros, burying the hook, generic titles, talking head without visual variety, no clear takeaway.

## Newsletter (Brevo)

**Energy:** Most intimate and personal. Letter to a smart friend.
**Register:** Warm, reflective, behind-the-scenes.

- Like texting a close friend who gets it
- Can be vulnerable, share doubts, show the real process
- Longest-form version of any thought
- One CTA only. Don't ask for ten things
- Plain text or minimal design. Substance over style
- Sent from hello@chromarabeauty.com

**Example tone:**
"This week was hard. We got rejected from [accelerator] and I spent two days wondering if we should pivot the whole positioning. Then I reread our original pitch doc from 2024 and remembered why we started this. The problem hasn't changed. 67% of people still can't find their shade. The solution we're building is still right. Sometimes you just need to remember the first principle."

**What works:** Founder vulnerability, behind-the-scenes updates, industry observations with personal take, exclusive previews, real numbers and real struggles.

**What fails:** Corporate newsletter voice, multiple CTAs, heavy design, treating subscribers like leads instead of community.

---

## Facebook

**Energy:** Warmest and most community-oriented. Inclusive energy.
**Register:** Friendly, conversational, inviting discussion.

- Older demographic. Slightly warmer than other platforms
- Ask questions. Invite participation
- Personal stories and relatable moments
- Native video preferred over links
- Links go in comments, not captions
- Groups are powerful here for community building

**Example post opening:**
"Question for you: when was the last time you bought a foundation and it actually matched on the first try? Not 'close enough.' Actually matched. I'm genuinely curious because in all my years on the Sephora floor, I could count those moments on one hand."

**What works:** Community questions, relatable struggles, founder stories, video content, celebrating shared experiences, warm engagement.

**What fails:** Cold brand voice, salesy language, ignoring comments, treating it like a broadcast channel instead of a conversation.

---

## Pinterest

**Energy:** Most aspirational and polished. Visual search engine mode.
**Register:** Clean, educational, SEO-conscious.

- This is a search engine, not social media. People come looking for solutions
- Vertical visuals (2:3 ratio), text overlay required for clarity
- SEO-rich descriptions with natural keyword integration
- Evergreen content. Pins compound over time
- Boards organized by search intent (shade matching, undertone guide, beauty tech)

**Example pin description:**
"67% of people can't find a foundation that matches. The problem isn't your undertone. It's how shades are manufactured. Here's what the beauty industry doesn't tell you about shade matching, and what's finally changing. Join the waitlist to be first in line for precision shade matching from 9 refillable pods."

**What works:** Clean visuals with readable text overlay, educational infographics, problem/solution framing, SEO keywords naturally woven in, consistent aesthetic across all pins.

**What fails:** Busy designs, text too small for thumbnails, no text overlay, missing descriptions, inconsistent posting.

## The Adaptation Rule

When adapting content across platforms:

1. **Change the tone first.** Same message, different energy level
2. **Change the format.** What works as a 60-second TikTok becomes a carousel, becomes a thread, becomes a newsletter section
3. **Change the hook.** What grabs attention differs by platform culture
4. **The voice stays the same.** Only the delivery changes

The core identity never shifts:
- We're founder-led, built from Sephora floor experience
- We understand both emotional frustration and structural failure
- We're building infrastructure, not another beauty brand
- We speak with calm intelligence and aspirational intimacy

## Platform Priority Order

For the "Donate $1 to End Shade Matching Forever" campaign and general content:

1. **TikTok** (write here first, highest priority, discovery engine)
2. **Instagram** (adapt from TikTok, polish for visual quality)
3. **YouTube** (long-form depth, Shorts from TikTok/Reels)
4. **X** (sharp punches, industry commentary, press discovery)
5. **Facebook** (community warmth, adapted from Instagram)
6. **Newsletter** (weekly digest, behind-the-scenes, deepest intimacy)
7. **Pinterest** (end of repurpose chain, most polished, most evergreen)

## Hard Rules That Apply Everywhere

No matter the platform, these constraints never change:

- [ ] No em dashes
- [ ] No antithetical parallelism ("this isn't X, it's Y")
- [ ] No rhetorical fragments for effect
- [ ] No oxidation/pH references
- [ ] No charity framing
- [ ] No investor language in consumer content
- [ ] No mechanism details (outputs only)
- [ ] Stats ceiling enforced: 67%, 40%, $2B+, 4.5M, 120B
- [ ] "NovaMirror" for consumers, never "ChromaCore"

See [[brand-voice]] for full constraint list and banned-patterns skill for structural patterns to avoid.
